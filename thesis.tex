\section{Typing rules}

We expand the syntax and typing of minimal semiringKanren to support polymorphic relations.

\begin{figure}
\[
\begin{array}{lrcl}
	\text{Programs} & P & ::= & \mathit{Rel}\dots \\
	\text{Relations} & Rel & ::= & \texttt{(defrel (\(R\) \(\forall \alpha \dots\) . (\(x:\tau\)) \(\dots\)) \(g\)) } \\
	\text{Goals} & g & ::= & \texttt{(conj \(g\) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(disj \(g\) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(fresh ((\(x:\tau\)) \(\dots\)) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(== \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(=/= \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(\(R\) \(v\) \(\dots\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(factor \(k\))} \\
	\text{Values} & v & ::= & \texttt{sole} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(left\(_{\tau_1, \tau_2}\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(right\(_{\tau_1, \tau_2}\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(pair \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & x \\
	\text{Types} & \tau & ::= & \texttt{Unit} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(Pair \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} \\
	& & | & \alpha \\
	\text{Relation names} & R & & \\
	\text{Variables} & x,y,z & & \\
	\text{Type variables} & \alpha,\beta,\gamma & & \\
	\text{Weights} & r & \in & \mathbb{K}
\end{array}
\]
\end{figure}

Here, the \(\Gamma\) context holds relation types, and the \(\Delta\) context holds variable types.
The \(\Delta \vdash v:\tau\) is the value (and therefore variable) typing judgement asserting that value \(v\) has type \(\tau\).
The \(\Gamma;\Delta \vdash g \wt\) judgement denotes that the goal \(g\) is well-typed under the \(\Gamma\) and \(\Delta\) contexts.
The \(\Gamma \vdash R \wt\) judgement denotes that the relation \(R\) is well-typed under the \(\Gamma\) context, and the \(P \wt\) judgement denotes that the entire program is well-typed.
The \(\Delta \vdash \tau \vt\) judgement denotes that the type \(\tau\) is valid under the \(\Delta\) context.
The judgements within the \(\Gamma\) context are extended to support type variables: \((R : \forall \alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m . \tau_1,\dots,\tau_n \rightarrow)\).
As before, \(\Delta\) holds value typing judgements (of the form \(x:\tau\)). 
\(Delta\) is also extended to hold judgements of the form \(\alpha : *\), indicating that the type variable \(\alpha\) can be treated as a valid type in the current context.
In both cases, note that type variables may now appear in \(\tau\).

Note that our relation typing judgement is "the relation is well-typed under a context" rather than "the relation has type \(\dots\)".
Relations are required to specify all needed typing information, so it only is necessary to check that their bodies are compatible.
Because the types of all relations are known ahead-of-time, we avoid potential issues of recursive dependencies when type-checking relation calls.
Also note that in the formal semantics type variables are explicitly specified, but in practice they can be automatically extracted by looking at the argument types.
Finally, note the use of a type variable substitution, \(\sigma\), when checking the type validity of relation calls.

We use the shorthand \((\vec x : \vec\tau)\) as shorthand for \((x_1 : \tau_1) \; \dots \; (x_n : \tau_n)\), or similar.
We also use \(\alpha \in \tau\) to say "the type variable \(\alpha\) occurs somewhere within type \(\tau\)."

\section{Monomorphizing semantics}

Here we update the denotational semantics to handle type variables.
Observe that when comparing values of an unknown type, we only need to know the size of the types.
Intuitively, the only comparison operations we have are equality and disequality, which result in diagonal or off-diagonal ndarrays depending only on size.
This generalizes to values which may only partially consist of an unknown type.
As such, for each relation \(R\) with type variables \(\alpha, \dots, \gamma\), we generate concrete instances corresponding to all possible type variable size assignments: \(R_{\alpha=1,\dots,\gamma=1}, \dots, R_{\alpha=n,\dots,\gamma=m}\).
A type of size \(n\) can easily be constructed as a sum of \(n\) \texttt{Unit}s.

As before, \(\eta\) is a relation environment and matches the relation type context.
\(\eta\) is a map from relation names to multidimensional arrays.
Similarly, \(\delta\) is the variable environment and matches the variable type context (excluding type variables).
\(\delta\) is a map from variable names to values.
As before, the denotation for types is a set of specific values.
Because types variables are reified in each relation (monomorphisation), no extra machinery is necessary.
The denotation for values is expressed as a specific primitive value constructed from \texttt{sole}, \texttt{(left \(v\))}, \texttt{(right \(v\))}, and \texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}, under a variable context.
The denotation for goals is expressed as a weight for each individual assignment of values to variables, but should be considered as a multidimensional array across all possible assignments.
We assume that all needed monomorphic instances of each relation are included in the full program before fixpointing.

\begin{figure}
\begin{align*}
	\den{\texttt{Unit}} &= \{\texttt{sole}\} \\
	\den{\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}} &= \{\texttt{(left \(v\))} \mid v \in \den{\tau_1}\} \cup \{\texttt{(right \(v\))} \mid v \in \den{\tau_2}\} \\
	\den{\texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}} &= \{\texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))} \mid v_1 \in \den{\tau_1}, v_2 \in \den{\tau_2}\}
\end{align*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for types.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\begin{align*}
	\den{\texttt{sole}} (\delta) &= \texttt{sole} \\
	\den{\texttt{(left \(v\))}} (\delta) &= \texttt{(left \(\den{v} (\delta) \))} \\
	\den{\texttt{(right \(v\))}} (\delta) &= \texttt{(right \(\den{v} (\delta) \))} \\
	\den{\texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}} (\delta) &= \texttt{(pair \(\den{v_1} (\delta) \) \(\den{v_2} (\delta)\))} \\
	\den{x} (\delta) &= \delta(x)
\end{align*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for values.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\begin{align*}
	\den{\texttt{(conj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))}} (\eta;\delta) &= \den{g_1} (\eta;\delta) \times \den{g_2} (\eta;\delta) \\
	\den{\texttt{(disj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))}} (\eta;\delta) &= \den{g_1} (\eta;\delta) + \den{g_2} (\eta;\delta) \\
	\den{\texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g\))}} (\eta;\delta) &= \sum_{v \in \den{\tau}} \den{g} (\eta; \delta\{x \mapsto v\}) \\
	\den{\texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}} (\eta; \delta) &=
	\begin{cases}
		1 & \text{if } \den{v_1} (\delta) = \den{v_2} (\delta) \\
		0 & \text{if } \den{v_1} (\delta) \ne \den{v_2} (\delta)
	\end{cases} \\
	\den{\texttt{(=/= \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}} (\eta; \delta) &=
	\begin{cases}
		1 & \text{if } \den{v_1} (\delta) \ne \den{v_2} (\delta) \\
		0 & \text{if } \den{v_1} (\delta) = \den{v_2} (\delta)
	\end{cases} \\
	\den{\texttt{(\(R\) \(\vec v\))}} (\eta; \delta) &= \eta(R_{\vec\alpha = |\sigma(\vec\alpha)|})(\den{\vec v}(\delta))
	\text{ where } \vec v : \sigma(\vec\tau) \\
	\den{\texttt{(factor \(r\))}} (\eta; \delta) &= r
\end{align*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for goals.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\begin{gather*}
	\den{\texttt{(defrel (\(R_{\vec \alpha = |\sigma(\vec \alpha)|}\) \(\forall \vec \alpha\) . (\(\vec x : \sigma (\vec \tau)\)) g)}} (\eta) =
	(\vec i) \mapsto \den{g} (\eta; \{\vec x \mapsto \den{\vec i}(\cdot) \} \\
	\den{\textit{Rel}_1, \dots, \textit{Rel}_Q} = \;
	\mathrel{\text{fix}} \eta \mathrel{\text{in}} \{R_1 \mapsto \den{\textit{Rel}_1} (\eta), \dots, R_Q \mapsto \den{\textit{Rel}_Q} (\eta) \}
\end{gather*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for relations and programs.}
\end{figure}

\begin{definition}
	We define a \emph{shell} using the following grammar:
	\[ \begin{array}{lrcl}
		\text{Shells} & s & ::= & \texttt{sole} \\
		& & | & \texttt{(left \(s\))} \\
		& & | & \texttt{(right \(s\))} \\
		& & | & \texttt{(pair \(s_1\) \(s_2\))} \\
		& & | & \texttt{(hole \(\alpha\))} \\
	\end{array} \]
\end{definition}

Shells are very similar to values, but holes must be a single, non-composite type variable.

Given a \emph{concrete value} with a corresponding type (including type variables), we can separate it into a shell and an ordered list of holes (corresponding to a single type variable) as follows:

\begin{align*}
	\shell_\texttt{Unit}(\texttt{sole}) &= \texttt{sole} \\
	\shell_\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}(\texttt{(left \(v\))}) &= \texttt{(left \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v)\))} \\
	\shell_\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}(\texttt{(right \(v\))}) &= \texttt{(right \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v)\))} \\
	\shell_\texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}(\texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}) &= \texttt{(pair \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v_1)\) \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v_2)\))} \\
	\shell_\alpha(v) &= \texttt{(hole \(\alpha\))}
\end{align*}

We use \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v)\) to denote the values of the holes corresponding to type variable \(\alpha\) within type \(\tau\).

\begin{align*}
	\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\texttt{sole}) &= \emptyset \\
	\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(\texttt{(left \(v\))}) &= \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v) \\
	\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(\texttt{(right \(v\))}) &= \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v) \\
	\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(\texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}) &= \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_1), \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_2) \\
	\holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v) &= v
\end{align*}

We also have versions of these functions which apply to \emph{value environments} (notated as \(\delta\) in the semantics) with corresponding type environments.

\begin{align*}
	\envshell_{\emptyset}() &= \emptyset \\
	\envshell_{\tau, \vec\tau}(x,\vec x \mapsto v, \vec v) &= x,\vec x \mapsto \shell_{\tau}(v), \envshell_{\vec\tau}(\vec v)
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
	\envholes_{\alpha \in \emptyset}() &= \emptyset \\
	\envholes_{\alpha \in \tau,\vec \tau}(x, \vec x \mapsto v, \vec v) &= \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v),\envholes_{\alpha \in \vec\tau}(\vec v)
\end{align*}

Note that extracted holes are not labeled; we can find the corresponding \texttt{(hole \(...\))} in the shell by an in-order search through the shell.

\begin{lemma}
	Let \(\Delta \vdash v_1 : \tau\) and \(\Delta \vdash v_2 : \tau\).
	Then \(v_1 = v_2\) iff \(\shell_\tau(v_1) = \shell_\tau(v_2)\) and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assuming \(v_1 = v_2\), we trivially have \(\shell_\tau(v_1) = \shell_\tau(v_2)\) and \(\holes_\alpha(v_1) = \holes_\alpha(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\) by substitution.

	Now assume \(\shell_\tau(v_1) = \shell_\tau(v_2)\) and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\), and aim to show \(v_1 = v_2\).
	Proof by induction on \(\Delta \vdash v_1 : \tau\) and \(\Delta \vdash v_2 : \tau\).
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Case \(\tau = \texttt{Unit}\).
			We have \(\shell_{\texttt{Unit}}(v_1) = \shell_{\texttt{Unit}}(v_2)\).
			This is only possible when \(v_1 = v_2 = \texttt{sole}\).
		\item Case \(\tau = \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}\).
			We have \(\shell_{\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \shell_{\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2)\).
			This is only possible in two cases.
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Subcase \(v_1 = \texttt{(left \(v_1'\))}\) and \(v_2 = \texttt{(left \(v_2'\))}\) where \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v_1') = \shell_{\tau_1}(v_2')\).
					We know \(\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\),
					so we can deduce by definition that \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_1') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_2') \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).
					By the inductive hypothesis, we now know \(v_1' = v_2'\).
					Thus \(\texttt{(left \(v_1'\))} = \texttt{(left \(v_2'\))}\), and so \(v_1 = v_2\).
				\item Subcase \(v_1 = \texttt{(right \(v_1'\))}\) and \(v_2 = \texttt{(right \(v_2'\))}\) where \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v_1') = \shell_{\tau_2}(v_2')\).
					We know \(\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\),
					so we can deduce by definition that \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_1') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_2') \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).
					By the inductive hypothesis, we now know \(v_1' = v_2'\).
					Thus \(\texttt{(right \(v_1'\))} = \texttt{(right \(v_2'\))}\), and so \(v_1 = v_2\).
			\end{itemize}
			In either case \(v_1 = v_2\).
		\item Case \(\tau = \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}\).
			We have \(\shell_{\texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \shell_{\texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1)\).
			This is only possible when \(v_1 = \texttt{(pair \(v_1'\) \(v_1''\))}\) and \(v_2 = \texttt{(pair \(v_2'\) \(v_2''\))}\),
			where \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v_1') = \shell_{\tau_1}(v_2')\) and \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v_1'') = \shell_{\tau_2}(v_2'')\).
			We also have \(\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2) \;
			\forall \alpha \in \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}\).
			Thus by definition of \(\holes\), we know: \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_1') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_2') \; \forall \alpha \in \tau_1\),
			and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_1'') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_2'') \; \forall \alpha \in \tau_2\).
			By the inductive hypothesis, we have \(v_1' = v_2'\) and \(v_1'' = v_2''\).
			Thus, \(\texttt{(pair \(v_1'\) \(v_1''\))} = \texttt{(pair \(v_2'\) \(v_2''\))}\), and so \(v_1 = v_2\).
		\item Case \(\tau = \alpha\).
			By definition, we know \(\holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v_1) = v_1\) and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v_2) = v_2\).
			By hypothesis, \(\holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v_2)\).
			Thus by transitivity of equality, \(v_1 = v_2\).
	\end{itemize}
	In any case, we have \(v_1 = v_2\).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
	We define \(\delta_1 \eqpata \delta_2\) when value environments \(\delta_1\) and \(\delta_2\) have the same \emph{equality pattern} for type variable \(\alpha\):
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \(\delta_1\) and \(\delta_2\) both have type environment \(\vec\tau\), and
		\item \(\envshell_{\vec\tau}(\delta_1) = \envshell_{\vec\tau}(\delta_2)\), and
		\item for all indices \(i, j\),
			holes \(h_1, k_1\) at those indices in \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \vec\tau}(\delta_1)\),
			and holes \(h_2, k_2\) at those indices in \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \vec\tau}(\delta_2)\),
			then \(h_1 = k_1\) and \(h_2 = k_2\), or \(h_1 \ne k_1\) and \(h_2 \ne k_2\).
	\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Note that because the envshells for each value environment are equal, both environments must have the same number of holes in the same locations; the third condition relies on the second condition.
The third condition boils down to "if pairs of holes are equal for one value environment, then they must also be equal for the other value environment" (and similar for disequality).

\begin{lemma}
	\(\eqpata\) is an equivalence relation.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	\(\eqpata\) is defined as separate conditions on shells and holes.
	The shell condition is direct equality, so \(\eqpata\) restricted soley to shells is clearly an equivalence relation.
	It remains to show that the \(\eqpata\) conditions for holes form an equivalence relation for value environments.

	\(\eqpata\) is obviously reflexive; for value environment \(\delta\), with holes \(h, k\), we either have \(h = k\) and \(h = k\), or \(h \ne k\) and \(h \ne k\).
	"And" is idempotent here, so this boils down to saying \(h = k\) or \(h \ne k\), which is clearly true.

	Let \(\delta_1 \eqpata \delta_2\) value environments with respective holes at indices \(i, j\): \(h_1, k_1\) and \(h_2, h_2\).
	We have \(h_1 = k_1\) and \(h_2 = k_2\), or \(h_1 \ne k_1\) and \(h_2 \ne \k_2\).
	"And" is symmetric, so we have \(h_2 = k_2\) and \(h_1 = k_1\), or \(h_2 \ne k_2\) and \(h_1 \ne \k_1\).
	This applies to all pairs of holes at any indices, this we have \(\delta_2 \eqpata \delta_1\), and so \(\eqpata\) is symmetric.

	Now assume \(\delta_1 \eqpata \delta_2\) and \(\delta_2 \eqpata \delta_3\), and aim to show \(\delta_1 \eqpata \delta_3\).
	Choose holes at indices \(i, j\) for each value environment: \(h_1, k_1\), \(h_2, k_2\), and \(h_3, k_3\).
	There are two cases to consider: \(h_1 = k_1\) and \(h_2 = k_2\) and \(h_3 = k_3\), or \(h_1 \ne k_1\) and \(h_2 \ne k_2\) and \(h_3 \ne k_3\).
	Any other cases do not satisfy the equality pattern precondition.
	In the first case, we have \(h_1 = k_1\) and \(h_3 = k_3\).
	In the second case, we have \(h_1 \ne k_1\) and \(h_3 \ne k_3\).
	This applies for any arbitrarily chosen holes of \(\delta_1, \delta_3\) at corresponding indices, thus we have \(d_1 \eqpata d_3\), and so \(\eqpata\) is transitive.

	\(\eqpata\) is defined as separate conditions on shells and holes.
	In either case, the conditions are reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.
	This, \(\eqpata\) is an equivalence relation.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
	We define the \emph{(maximum) number of occurrences of type variable \(\alpha\)} in type \(\tau\), denoted \(\#_\alpha \tau\), as follows:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{Unit} = 0\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} = \max(\#_\alpha \tau_1, \#_\alpha \tau_2)\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} = \#_\alpha \tau_1 + \#_\alpha \tau_2\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \alpha = 1\)
	\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

More specifically, this is the maximum number of \(\alpha\)-typed sub-values that can occur in a value of type \(\tau\).
This generalizes cleanly to type environments:

\begin{definition}
	We define the \emph{(maximum) number of occurrences of type variable \(\alpha\)} in type environment \(\vec\tau\), denoted \(\#_\alpha \vec\tau\), as follows:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \(\#_\alpha \emptyset = 0\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \tau, \vec\tau = \#_\alpha \tau + \#_\alpha \vec\tau\)
	\end{itemize}
\end{definition}
