\chapter{Polymorphic semiringKanren}

\section{Typing rules}

We expand the syntax and typing of minimal semiringKanren to support polymorphic relations.

\begin{figure}
\[
\begin{array}{lrcl}
	\text{Programs} & P & ::= & \mathit{Rel}\dots \\
	\text{Relations} & Rel & ::= & \texttt{(defrel (\(R\) \(\forall \alpha \dots\) . (\(x:\tau\)) \(\dots\)) \(g\)) } \\
	\text{Goals} & g & ::= & \texttt{(conj \(g\) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(disj \(g\) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(fresh ((\(x:\tau\)) \(\dots\)) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(== \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(=/= \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(\(R\) \(v\) \(\dots\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(factor \(k\))} \\
	\text{Values} & v & ::= & \texttt{sole} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(left\(_{\tau_1, \tau_2}\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(right\(_{\tau_1, \tau_2}\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(pair \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & x \\
	\text{Types} & \tau & ::= & \texttt{Unit} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(Sum \(\tau\) \(\tau\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(Pair \(\tau\) \(\tau\))} \\
	& & | & \alpha \\
	\text{Relation names} & R & & \\
	\text{Variables} & x,y,z & & \\
	\text{Type variables} & \alpha,\beta,\gamma & & \\
	\text{Weights} & r & \in & \mathbb{K}
\end{array}
\]
\end{figure}

Here, the \(\Gamma\) context holds relation types, and the \(\Delta\) context holds variable types.
The \(\Delta \vdash v:\tau\) is the value (and therefore variable) typing judgement asserting that value \(v\) has type \(\tau\).
The \(\Gamma;\Delta \vdash g \wt\) judgement denotes that the goal \(g\) is well-typed under the \(\Gamma\) and \(\Delta\) contexts.
The \(\Gamma \vdash R \wt\) judgement denotes that the relation \(R\) is well-typed under the \(\Gamma\) context, and the \(P \wt\) judgement denotes that the entire program is well-typed.
The \(\Delta \vdash \tau \vt\) judgement denotes that the type \(\tau\) is valid under the \(\Delta\) context.
The judgements within the \(\Gamma\) context are extended to support type variables: \((R : \forall \alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m . \tau_1,\dots,\tau_n \rightarrow)\).
As before, \(\Delta\) holds value typing judgements (of the form \(x:\tau\)). 
\(Delta\) is also extended to hold judgements of the form \(\alpha : *\), indicating that the type variable \(\alpha\) can be treated as a valid type in the current context.
In both cases, note that type variables may now appear in \(\tau\).

Note that our relation typing judgement is "the relation is well-typed under a context" rather than "the relation has type \(\dots\)".
Relations are required to specify all needed typing information, so it only is necessary to check that their bodies are compatible.
Because the types of all relations are known ahead-of-time, we avoid potential issues of recursive dependencies when type-checking relation calls.
Also note that in the formal semantics type variables are explicitly specified, but in practice they can be automatically extracted by looking at the argument types.
Finally, note the use of a type variable substitution, \(\sigma\), when checking the type validity of relation calls.

\section{"Infinite" semantics}

Here we update the denotational semantics to handle type variables.
Observe that when comparing values of an unknown type, we only need to know the size of the types.
Intuitively, the only comparison operations we have are equality and disequality, which result in diagonal or off-diagonal ndarrays depending only on size.
This generalizes to values which may only partially consist of an unknown type.
As such, for each relation \(R\) with type variables \(\alpha, \dots, \gamma\), we generate concrete instances corresponding to all possible type variable size assignments: \(R_{\alpha=1,\dots,\gamma=1}, \dots, R_{\alpha=n,\dots,\gamma=m}\).
A type of size \(n\) can easily be constructed as a sum of \(n\) \texttt{Unit}s.

As before, \(\eta\) is a relation environment and matches the relation type context.
\(\eta\) is a map from relation names to multidimensional arrays.
Similarly, \(\delta\) is the variable environment and matches the variable type context (excluding type variables).
\(\delta\) is a map from variable names to values.
As before, the denotation for types is a set of specific values.
Because types variables are reified in each relation (monomorphisation), no extra machinery is necessary.
The denotation for values is expressed as a specific primitive value constructed from \texttt{sole}, \texttt{(left \(v\))}, \texttt{(right \(v\))}, and \texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}, under a variable context.
The denotation for goals is expressed as a weight for each individual assignment of values to variables, but should be considered as a multidimensional array across all possible assignments.

\begin{figure}
\begin{align*}
	\llbracket \texttt{Unit} \rrbracket &= \{\texttt{sole}\} \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} \rrbracket &= \{\texttt{(left \(v\))} \mid v \in \llbracket \tau_1 \rrbracket\} \cup \{\texttt{(right \(v\))} \mid v \in \llbracket \tau_2 \rrbracket\} \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} \rrbracket &= \{\texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))} \mid v_1 \in \llbracket \tau_1 \rrbracket, v_2 \in \llbracket \tau_2 \rrbracket\}
\end{align*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for types.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\begin{align*}
	\llbracket \texttt{sole} \rrbracket (\delta) &= \texttt{sole} \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(left \(v\))} \rrbracket (\delta) &= \texttt{(left \(\llbracket v \rrbracket (\delta) \))} \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(right \(v\))} \rrbracket (\delta) &= \texttt{(right \(\llbracket v \rrbracket (\delta) \))} \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))} \rrbracket (\delta) &= \texttt{(pair \(\llbracket v_1 \rrbracket (\delta) \) \(\llbracket v_2 \rrbracket (\delta)\))} \\
	\llbracket x \rrbracket (\delta) &= \delta(x)
\end{align*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for values.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\begin{align*}
	\llbracket \texttt{(conj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))} \rrbracket (\eta;\delta) &= \llbracket g_1 \rrbracket (\eta;\delta) \times \llbracket g_2 \rrbracket (\eta;\delta) \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(disj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))} \rrbracket (\eta;\delta) &= \llbracket g_1 \rrbracket (\eta;\delta) + \llbracket g_2 \rrbracket (\eta;\delta) \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g\))} \rrbracket (\eta;\delta) &= \sum_{v \in \llbracket \tau \rrbracket} \llbracket g \rrbracket (\eta; \delta\{x \mapsto v\}) \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))} \rrbracket (\eta; \delta) &=
	\begin{cases}
		1 & \text{if } \llbracket v_1 \rrbracket (\delta) = \llbracket v_2 \rrbracket (\delta) \\
		0 & \text{if } \llbracket v_1 \rrbracket (\delta) \ne \llbracket v_2 \rrbracket (\delta)
	\end{cases} \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(=/= \(v_1\) \(v_2\))} \rrbracket (\eta; \delta) &=
	\begin{cases}
		1 & \text{if } \llbracket v_1 \rrbracket (\delta) \ne \llbracket v_2 \rrbracket (\delta) \\
		0 & \text{if } \llbracket v_1 \rrbracket (\delta) = \llbracket v_2 \rrbracket (\delta)
	\end{cases} \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(\(R\) \(v_1\) \(\dots\) \(v_n\))} \rrbracket (\eta; \delta) &= \eta(R_{\alpha_1=|\sigma(\alpha_1)|,\dots,\alpha_m=|\sigma(s_m)|})(\delta(v_1), \dots, \delta(v_n))
	\text{ where } v_i : \sigma(\tau_i) \\
	\llbracket \texttt{(factor \(r\))} \rrbracket (\eta; \delta) &= r
\end{align*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for goals.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\begin{gather*}
	\llbracket \texttt{(defrel (\(R_{\alpha_1=|\sigma(\alpha_1)|, \dots, \alpha_m = |\sigma(\alpha_m)|}\) \(\forall \alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m\) .
	(\(x_1 : \sigma(\tau_1)\)) \(\dots\) (\(x_n : \sigma(\tau_n)\))) \(g\))} \rrbracket (\eta) = \\
	(i_1, \dots, i_n) \mapsto \llbracket g \rrbracket (\eta; \{x_1 \mapsto \llbracket i_1 \rrbracket(\cdot), \dots, x_n \mapsto \llbracket i_n \rrbracket(\cdot)\} \\
	\llbracket \textit{Rel}_1, \dots, \textit{Rel}_Q \rrbracket =
	\mu \delta . \{R_1 \mapsto \llbracket \textit{Rel}_1 \rrbracket (\delta), \dots, R_Q \mapsto \llbracket \textit{Rel}_Q \rrbracket (\delta) \}
\end{gather*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for relations and programs.}
\end{figure}
