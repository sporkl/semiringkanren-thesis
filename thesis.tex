\section{Typing rules}

We expand the syntax and typing of minimal semiringKanren to support polymorphic relations.

\begin{figure}
\[
\begin{array}{lrcl}
	\text{Programs} & P & ::= & \mathit{Rel}\dots \\
	\text{Relations} & Rel & ::= & \texttt{(defrel (\(R\) \(\forall \alpha \dots\) . (\(x:\tau\)) \(\dots\)) \(g\)) } \\
	\text{Goals} & g & ::= & \texttt{(conj \(g\) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(disj \(g\) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(fresh ((\(x:\tau\)) \(\dots\)) \(g\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(== \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(=/= \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(\(R\) \(v\) \(\dots\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(factor \(k\))} \\
	\text{Values} & v & ::= & \texttt{sole} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(left\(_{\tau_1, \tau_2}\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(right\(_{\tau_1, \tau_2}\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(pair \(v\) \(v\))} \\
	& & | & x \\
	\text{Types} & \tau & ::= & \texttt{Unit} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} \\
	& & | & \texttt{(Pair \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} \\
	& & | & \alpha \\
	\text{Relation names} & R & & \\
	\text{Variables} & x,y,z & & \\
	\text{Type variables} & \alpha,\beta & & \\
	\text{Weights} & r & \in & \mathbb{K}
\end{array}
\]
\end{figure}

Here, the \(\Gamma\) context holds relation types, and the \(\Delta\) context holds variable types.
The \(\Delta \vdash v:\tau\) is the value (and therefore variable) typing judgement asserting that value \(v\) has type \(\tau\).
The \(\Gamma;\Delta \vdash g \wt\) judgement denotes that the goal \(g\) is well-typed under the \(\Gamma\) and \(\Delta\) contexts.
The \(\Gamma \vdash R \wt\) judgement denotes that the relation \(R\) is well-typed under the \(\Gamma\) context, and the \(P \wt\) judgement denotes that the entire program is well-typed.
The \(\Delta \vdash \tau \vt\) judgement denotes that the type \(\tau\) is valid under the \(\Delta\) context.
The judgements within the \(\Gamma\) context are extended to support type variables: \((R : \forall \alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m . \tau_1,\dots,\tau_n \rightarrow)\).
As before, \(\Delta\) holds value typing judgements (of the form \(x:\tau\)). 
\(\Delta\) is also extended to hold judgements of the form \(\alpha : *\), indicating that the type variable \(\alpha\) can be treated as a valid type in the current context.
In both cases, note that type variables may now appear in \(\tau\).

Note that our relation typing judgement is "the relation is well-typed under a context" rather than "the relation has type \(\dots\)".
Relations are required to specify all needed typing information, so it only is necessary to check that their bodies are compatible.
Because the types of all relations are known ahead-of-time, we avoid potential issues of recursive dependencies when type-checking relation calls.
Also note that in the formal semantics type variables are explicitly specified, but in practice they can be automatically extracted by looking at the argument types.
Finally, note the use of a type variable substitution, \(\sigma\), when checking the validity of types which may contain not-yet-valid type variables (such as relation calls).

We use the shorthand \((\vec x : \vec\tau)\) as shorthand for \((x_1 : \tau_1) \; \dots \; (x_n : \tau_n)\), or similar.
We also use \(\alpha \in \tau\) to say "the type variable \(\alpha\) occurs somewhere within type \(\tau\)."

\section{Monomorphizing semantics}

Here we update the denotational semantics to handle type variables.
We use the term "concrete types" for types which do not contain type variables.
We introduce \emph{type variable substitutions}, which we apply to a type to turn it into a concrete type, by replacing type variables with specific concrete types.

Temporarily ignoring substitutions, imagine we wish to operate on values of an unknown type.
In order to represent such a value with the array-based semantics, we need to know the size of the type so that we can have a large enough corresponding array dimension.
Once we have space for the type, we can easily define equality and disequality operations on the type by using diagonals and off-diagonals, as before.
This generalizes to values which may only partially consist of an unknown type.
But because the specifics of the types are unknown, we have no other operations.
Thus, we can treat all unknown types of the same size equivalently.
As such, for each relation \(R\) with type variables \(\alpha, \dots, \gamma\),
we generate concrete instances of these relations \(R_{\sigma_1}, \dots, R_{\sigma_n}\), corresponding to all unique-up-to-size type variable substitutions \(\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_n\).
Note that we can easily generate types of size \(n\) as a sum type of \(n\) \(\texttt{Unit}\)s, so generating these substitutions is straightforward.

As before, \(\gamma\) is a relation environment and matches the relation type context.
\(\gamma\) is a map from relation names to multidimensional arrays.
Similarly, \(\delta\) is the variable environment with type \(\sigma(\Delta)\).
\(\delta\) is a map from variable names to values.
As before, the denotation for types is a set of specific values.
We handle type variables by applying \(\sigma\) before taking the denotation of a type.
We use the term "concrete value" to refer to values constructed from \texttt{sole}, \texttt{(left \(v\))}, \texttt{(right \(v\))}, and \texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}; concrete values can not be variables.
The denotation for values under a variable context is a specific concrete value.
The denotation for goals is expressed as a weight for each individual assignment of values to variables, but should be considered as a multidimensional array across all possible assignments.
We assume that all needed monomorphic instances of each relation are included in the full program before fixpointing.

\begin{align*}
	\den{\texttt{Unit}} &= \{\texttt{sole}\} \\
	\den{\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}} &= \{\texttt{(left \(v\))} \mid v \in \den{\tau_1}\} \cup \{\texttt{(right \(v\))} \mid v \in \den{\tau_2}\} \\
	\den{\texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}} &= \{\texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))} \mid v_1 \in \den{\tau_1}, v_2 \in \den{\tau_2}\} \\
\end{align*}

	\den{\texttt{(right \(v\))}} (\delta) &= \texttt{(right \(\den{v} (\delta) \))} \\

\begin{figure}
\begin{align*}
	\den{\texttt{(conj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))}} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) &= \den{g_1} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) \times \den{g_2} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) \\
	\den{\texttt{(disj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))}} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) &= \den{g_1} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) + \den{g_2} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) \\
	\den{\texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g\))}} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) &= \sum_{v \in \den{\sigma(\tau)}} \den{g} (\gamma;\delta,x \mapsto v;\sigma) \\
	\den{\texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) &=
	\begin{cases}
		1 & \text{if } \den{v_1} (\delta) = \den{v_2} (\delta) \\
		0 & \text{if } \den{v_1} (\delta) \ne \den{v_2} (\delta)
	\end{cases} \\
	\den{\texttt{(=/= \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) &=
	\begin{cases}
		1 & \text{if } \den{v_1} (\delta) \ne \den{v_2} (\delta) \\
		0 & \text{if } \den{v_1} (\delta) = \den{v_2} (\delta)
	\end{cases} \\

	\text{ where } \vec v : \sigma'(\vec\tau) \\
	\den{\texttt{(factor \(r\))}} (\gamma;\delta;\sigma) &= r
\end{align*}
\caption{Denotational semantics for goals.}
\end{figure}

\section{Non-monomorphizing semantics correctness}

\begin{definition}
	We define a \emph{shell} using the following grammar:
	\[ \begin{array}{lrcl}
		\text{Shells} & s & ::= & \texttt{sole} \\
		& & | & \texttt{(left \(s\))} \\
		& & | & \texttt{(right \(s\))} \\
		& & | & \texttt{(pair \(s_1\) \(s_2\))} \\
		& & | & \texttt{(hole \(\alpha\))} \\
	\end{array} \]
\end{definition}

Shells are very similar to values, but additionally have holes which are associated with a single, non-composite type variable.

Given a concrete value with a corresponding type (including type variables), we can separate it into a shell and an ordered list of holes (corresponding to a single type variable) as follows:

\begin{align*}
	\shell_\texttt{Unit}(\texttt{sole}) &= \texttt{sole} \\
	\shell_\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}(\texttt{(left \(v\))}) &= \texttt{(left \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v)\))} \\
	\shell_\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}(\texttt{(right \(v\))}) &= \texttt{(right \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v)\))} \\
	\shell_\texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}(\texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}) &= \texttt{(pair \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v_1)\) \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v_2)\))} \\
	\shell_\alpha(v) &= \texttt{(hole \(\alpha\))}
\end{align*}

We use \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v)\) to denote the values of the holes corresponding to type variable \(\alpha\) within type \(\tau\).

\begin{align*}
	\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\texttt{sole}) &= \emptyset \\
	\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(\texttt{(left \(v\))}) &= \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v) \\
	\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(\texttt{(right \(v\))}) &= \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v) \\
	\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(\texttt{(pair \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}) &= \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_1), \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_2) \\
	\holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v) &= v \\
	\holes_{\beta \in \alpha}(v) &= \emptyset
\end{align*}

We also have versions of these functions which apply to \emph{value environments} (notated as \(\delta\) in the semantics) with corresponding type environments.

\begin{align*}
	\envshell_{\emptyset}() &= \emptyset \\
	\envshell_{\Delta, \tau}(\vec x \mapsto \vec v, x \mapsto v) &= \envshell_{\Delta}(\vec x \mapsto \vec v), x \mapsto \shell_{\tau}(v)
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
	\envholes_{\alpha \in \emptyset}() &= \emptyset \\
	\envholes_{\Delta, \alpha \in \tau}(\vec x \mapsto \vec v, x \mapsto v) &= \envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta}(\vec v), \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v)
\end{align*}

Note that extracted holes are not labeled; we can find the corresponding \texttt{(hole \(...\))} in the shell by an in-order search through the shell.

\begin{lemma}
	\label{lem:values-shells-holes}
	Let \(\Delta \vdash v_1 : \tau\) and \(\Delta \vdash v_2 : \tau\).
	Then \(v_1 = v_2\) iff \(\shell_\tau(v_1) = \shell_\tau(v_2)\) and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assuming \(v_1 = v_2\), we trivially have \(\shell_\tau(v_1) = \shell_\tau(v_2)\) and \(\holes_\alpha(v_1) = \holes_\alpha(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\) by substitution.

	Now assume \(\shell_\tau(v_1) = \shell_\tau(v_2)\) and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\), and aim to show \(v_1 = v_2\).
	Proof by induction on \(\Delta \vdash v_1 : \tau\) and \(\Delta \vdash v_2 : \tau\).
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Case: \(\tau = \texttt{Unit}\).
			We have \(\shell_{\texttt{Unit}}(v_1) = \shell_{\texttt{Unit}}(v_2)\).
			This is only possible when \(v_1 = v_2 = \texttt{sole}\).
		\item Case: \(\tau = \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}\).
			We have \(\shell_{\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \shell_{\texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2)\).
			This is only possible in two cases.
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Subcase: \(v_1 = \texttt{(left \(v_1'\))}\) and \(v_2 = \texttt{(left \(v_2'\))}\) where \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v_1') = \shell_{\tau_1}(v_2')\).
					We know \(\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\),
					so we can deduce by definition that \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_1') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_2') \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).
					By the inductive hypothesis, we now know \(v_1' = v_2'\).
					Thus \(\texttt{(left \(v_1'\))} = \texttt{(left \(v_2'\))}\), and so \(v_1 = v_2\).
				\item Subcase: \(v_1 = \texttt{(right \(v_1'\))}\) and \(v_2 = \texttt{(right \(v_2'\))}\) where \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v_1') = \shell_{\tau_2}(v_2')\).
					We know \(\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\),
					so we can deduce by definition that \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_1') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_2') \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).
					By the inductive hypothesis, we now know \(v_1' = v_2'\).
					Thus \(\texttt{(right \(v_1'\))} = \texttt{(right \(v_2'\))}\), and so \(v_1 = v_2\).
			\end{itemize}
			In either case \(v_1 = v_2\).
		\item Case: \(\tau = \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}\).
			We have \(\shell_{\texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \shell_{\texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2)\).
			This is only possible when \(v_1 = \texttt{(pair \(v_1'\) \(v_1''\))}\) and \(v_2 = \texttt{(pair \(v_2'\) \(v_2''\))}\),
			where \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v_1') = \shell_{\tau_1}(v_2')\) and \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v_1'') = \shell_{\tau_2}(v_2'')\).
			We also have \(\holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}}(v_2) \;
			\forall \alpha \in \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))}\).
			By definition of \(\holes\), we therefore have: \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_1'),\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_1'') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_2'),\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_2'')\).
			Because \(\shell_{\tau_1}(v'_1) = \shell_{\tau_1}(v'_2)\) and \(\shell_{\tau_2}(v''_1) = \shell_{\tau_2}(v''_2)\),
			we can count holes to deduce: \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_1') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_1}(v_2') \; \forall \alpha \in \tau_1\),
			and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_1'') = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau_2}(v_2'') \; \forall \alpha \in \tau_2\).
			By the inductive hypothesis, we have \(v_1' = v_2'\) and \(v_1'' = v_2''\).
			Thus, \(\texttt{(pair \(v_1'\) \(v_1''\))} = \texttt{(pair \(v_2'\) \(v_2''\))}\), and so \(v_1 = v_2\).
		\item Case: \(\tau = \alpha\) (for any type variable).
			Our hypothesis holds for all type variables, so we know \(\holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v_1) = v_1\) and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v_2) = v_2\).
			By hypothesis, \(\holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v_1) = \holes_{\alpha \in \alpha}(v_2)\).
			Thus by transitivity of equality, \(v_1 = v_2\).
	\end{itemize}
	In any case, we have \(v_1 = v_2\).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
	\label{def:eqpat}
	We define \(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_2\) when value environments \(\delta_1\) and \(\delta_2\) have the same \emph{equality pattern} under type environment \(\Delta\).
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \(\delta_1 : \sigma_1(\Delta)\) and \(\delta_2 : \sigma_2(\Delta)\), for some type variable substitutions \(\sigma_1, \sigma_2\).
		\item \(\envshell_{\Delta}(\delta_1) = \envshell_{\Delta}(\delta_2)\), and
		\item for all type variables \(\alpha \in \Delta\), for all indices \(i, j\),
			holes \(h_1, k_1\) at those indices in \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta}(\delta_1)\),
			and holes \(h_2, k_2\) at those indices in \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta}(\delta_2)\),
			then \(h_1 = k_1\) and \(h_2 = k_2\), or \(h_1 \ne k_1\) and \(h_2 \ne k_2\).
	\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Note that because the envshells for each value environment are equal, both environments must have the same number of holes in the same locations; the third condition relies on the second condition.
The third condition boils down to "if pairs of holes are equal for one value environment, then they must also be equal for the other value environment" (and similar for disequality).

\begin{lemma}
	\label{lem:eqpat-equivrel}
	\(\eqpatD\) is an equivalence relation.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	\(\eqpatD\) is defined as separate conditions on shells and holes.
	The shell condition is direct equality, so it is clearly an equivalence relation.
	It remains to show that the \(\eqpatD\) conditions for holes forms an equivalence relation for value environments.

	\(\eqpatD\) is obviously reflexive; for holes \(h, k\) at type \(\alpha\), we either have \(h = k\) and \(h = k\), or \(h \ne k\) and \(h \ne k\).
	This boils down to saying \(h = k\) or \(h \ne k\), which is clearly true.

	Let \(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_2\) be value environments with respective holes at indices \(i, j\): \(h_1, k_1\) and \(h_2, h_2\), for type \(\alpha\).
	We have \(h_1 = k_1\) and \(h_2 = k_2\), or \(h_1 \ne k_1\) and \(h_2 \ne k_2\).
	"And" is symmetric, so we have \(h_2 = k_2\) and \(h_1 = k_1\), or \(h_2 \ne k_2\) and \(h_1 \ne k_1\).
	This applies to all pairs of holes at any indices, at for any type variable, thus we have \(\delta_2 \eqpatD \delta_1\), and so \(\eqpatD\) is symmetric.

	Now assume \(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_2\) and \(\delta_2 \eqpatD \delta_3\), and aim to show \(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_3\).
	Choose type variable \(\alpha\), and \(\alpha\)-holes at indices \(i, j\) for each value environment: \(h_1, k_1\), \(h_2, k_2\), and \(h_3, k_3\).
	There are two cases to consider: \(h_1 = k_1\) and \(h_2 = k_2\) and \(h_3 = k_3\), or \(h_1 \ne k_1\) and \(h_2 \ne k_2\) and \(h_3 \ne k_3\).
	Any other cases do not satisfy the equality pattern precondition.
	In the first case, we have \(h_1 = k_1\) and \(h_3 = k_3\).
	In the second case, we have \(h_1 \ne k_1\) and \(h_3 \ne k_3\).
	This applies for any arbitrarily chosen \(\alpha\)-holes of \(\delta_1, \delta_3\) at corresponding indices, for any \(\alpha\), thus we have \(d_1 \eqpatD d_3\), and so \(\eqpatD\) is transitive.

	\(\eqpatD\) is defined as separate conditions on shells and holes.
	Both conditions are reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.
	This, \(\eqpatD\) is an equivalence relation.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
	\label{lem:eqpat-substitution}
	Let \(\delta_1, \delta_2\) be value environments where \(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_2\).
	Additionally, let \(v_1, v_2 : \tau\) be values.
	\(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) = \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\) iff \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) = \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\), and
	\(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) \ne \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\) iff \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) \ne \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Note that we only need to prove one direction for each "iff"; the other direction holds by symmetry.
	First assume \(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) = \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\) and aim to prove \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) = \den{v_2}(\delta_2))\).

	By lemma \ref{lem:values-shells-holes}, we know \(\shell_{\tau}(\den{v_1}(\delta_1)) = \shell_{\tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_1))\)
	and \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_1}(\delta_1)) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_1)) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).
	\(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_2\), so \(\envshell_\Delta(\delta_1) = \envshell_\Delta(\delta_2)\).
	% do I need to explain this next sentence better?
	Thus \(\shell_\tau(\den{v_1}(\delta_1)) = \shell_\tau(\den{v_1}(\delta_2))\) and \(\shell_\tau(\den{v_2}(\delta_1)) = \shell_\tau(\den{v_2}(\delta_2))\).
	By transitivity of equality, we therefore know \(\shell_\tau(\den{v_1}(\delta_2)) = \shell_{\tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_2))\).

	Choose \(\alpha \in \tau\).
	We also may deduce by lemma \ref{lem:values-shells-holes} that \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_1}(\delta_1)) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_1))\).
	We know \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) have the same shells under \(\delta_1\),
	so we can choose holes \(h_1 \in \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_1}(\delta_1))\) at index \(i\), and \(k_1 \in \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_1))\) at index \(j\) where \(i = j\).
	So holes \(h\) and \(k\) are at the same index \(i = j\) within their respective value shells.
	Because of this, and because \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_1}(\delta_1)) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_1))\), we know \(h_1 = k_1\).
	We can determine the indices \(i_\delta, j_\delta\) of \(h_1, k_1\) in \(\delta_1\) by considering the positions of variables within \(v_1\) and \(v_2\), and counting accordingly.
	\(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_2\), thus \(h_2 = k_2\) for holes \(h_2, k_2\) at indices \(i_\delta, j_\delta\) in \(\delta_2\).
	Because we know \(\envshell_\Delta(\delta_1) = \envshell_{\Delta}(\delta_2)\), we know holes \(h_2, k_2\) are at the same indices \(i = j\) within the value shells of \(v_1, v_2\).
	\(h_2 = k_2\) holds for any chosen indices \(i = j\) and type variable \(\alpha\),
	hence \(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_1}(\delta_2)) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).

	We have shown \(\shell_\tau(\den{v_1}(\delta_2)) = \shell_{\tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_2))\) and
	\(\holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_1}(\delta_2)) = \holes_{\alpha \in \tau}(\den{v_2}(\delta_2) \; \forall \alpha \in \tau\).
	Thus by lemma \ref{lem:values-shells-holes}, \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) = \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\).

	Now assume \(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) \ne \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\)
	Suppose towards a contradiction \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) = \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\).
	We have just shown that if we assume this is true, we then have \(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) = \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\).
	But this contradicts our assumption.
	So we must have \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) \ne \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
	We define the \emph{(maximum) number of occurrences of type variable \(\alpha\)} in type \(\tau\), denoted \(\#_\alpha \tau\), as follows:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{Unit} = 0\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(Sum \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} = \max(\#_\alpha \tau_1, \#_\alpha \tau_2)\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(Prod \(\tau_1\) \(\tau_2\))} = \#_\alpha \tau_1 + \#_\alpha \tau_2\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \alpha = 1\)
	\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

More specifically, this is the maximum number of \(\alpha\)-typed holes that can occur in a value of type \(\tau\).

\begin{definition}
	We define the \emph{(maximum) number of occurrences of type variable \(\alpha\)} in type environment \(\Delta\), denoted \(\#_\alpha \Delta\), as follows:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \(\#_\alpha \emptyset = 0\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \tau, \Delta = \#_\alpha \tau + \#_\alpha \Delta\)
	\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
	We define the \emph{(maximum) number of occurrences of type variable \(\alpha\) in goal \(g\) under type environment \(\Delta\)}, denoted \(\#_\alpha g(\Delta)\), inductively:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(conj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))}(\Delta) = \max(\#_\alpha g_1, \#_\alpha g_2)\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(disj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))}(\Delta) = \max(\#_\alpha g_1, \#_\alpha g_2)\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g\))}(\Delta) = \#_\alpha g(\Delta,x:\tau)\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}(\Delta) = \#_\alpha \Delta\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(=/= \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}(\Delta) = \#_\alpha \Delta\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(\(R\) \(\vec v\))}(\Delta) = \#_\alpha \Delta\)
		\item \(\#_\alpha \texttt{(factor \(r\))}(\Delta) = \#_\alpha \Delta\)
	\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Which makes it easy to count for relations:

\begin{definition}
	We define the \emph{(maximum) number of occurrences of type variable \(\alpha\) in relation \(R\)}, denoted \(\#_\alpha R\),
	as the (maximum) of occurrences of \(\alpha\) in the top-level goal, with the type environment determined from the arguments.
	\begin{align*}
		\#_\alpha \texttt{(defrel (\(R\) \(\forall \alpha \dots\) (\(x_1:\tau_1\)) \dots (\(x_n : \tau_1\))) \(g\))} = \#_\alpha g(x_1:\tau_1,\dots,x_n:\tau_n)
	\end{align*}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
	We define the \emph{size of type \(\tau\)}, notated \(|\tau|\), to be the number of values of type \(\tau\).
	\(\tau\) is undefined when \(\tau\) contains a type variable.
	\begin{align*}
		|\tau| = | \den{\tau} |
	\end{align*}
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}
	\label{lem:eqpat-extend}
	Let \(\Delta \vdash v_1 : \sigma_1(\tau)\).
	If \(|\sigma_2(\alpha)| \ge \#_\alpha \Delta, \tau \; \forall \alpha \in \Delta\),
	and \(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_2\),
	then there exists \(\Delta \vdash v_2 : \sigma_2(\tau)\) such that \(\delta_1, x \mapsto v_1 \eqpat_{\Delta, \tau} \delta_2, x \mapsto v_2\).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	For the equality pattern to continue to hold, we must have \(\shell(v_1) = \shell(v_2)\).
	It remains to find appropriate values for the holes of \(v_2\).

	Choose \(\alpha \in \tau\), and let \(i\) be the smallest index such that \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta, \tau}(\delta_2)[i]\) does not yet have an assigned value.
	For \(\delta_1\), try to find index \(j < i\) such that \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta, \tau}(\delta_1)[i] = \envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta, \tau}(\delta_1)[j]\).
	If such \(j\) exists, assign the value of \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta, \tau}(\delta_2)[j]\) to \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta, \tau}(\delta_2)[i]\).
	If such \(j\) does not exist, choose an \(\sigma_2(\tau)\)-typed value which does not yet occur in \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta, \tau}(\delta_2)\),
	and assign it to \(\envholes_{\alpha \in \Delta, \tau}(\delta_2)[i]\).
	Note that it is always possible to find such a value: \(|\sigma_2(\alpha)| \ge \#_\alpha \Delta,\tau\),
	so \(\sigma_2(\alpha)\) has at least as many distinct values as there are \(\alpha\)-typed holes.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
	Let \(g\) be a goal, \(\gamma\) be a relation environment, and \(\delta_1, \delta_2\) be value environments.
	If \(\delta_1 \eqpatD \delta_2\),
	\(|\sigma_1(\alpha)| \ge \#_\alpha g(\Delta) \; \forall \alpha \in \Delta\),
	\(|\sigma_2(\alpha)| \ge \#_\alpha g(\Delta) \; \forall \alpha \in \Delta\),
	and \(+\) is idempotent (\(a + a = a\)), then
	\(\den{g}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
	Proof by induction on the goal structure of \(g\).
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \(g = \texttt{(conj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))}\).
			By the inductive hypothesis, \(\den{g_1}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g_1}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\) and \(\den{g_2}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) = \den{g_2}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\)
			Thus \(\den{g_1}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) * \den{g_2}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g_1}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) * \den{g_2}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\),
			and so \(\den{g}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\).
		\item \(g = \texttt{(disj \(g_1\) \(g_2\))}\).
			By the inductive hypothesis, \(\den{g_1}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g_1}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\) and \(\den{g_2}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) = \den{g_2}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\)
			Thus \(\den{g_1}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) + \den{g_2}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g_1}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) + \den{g_2}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\),
			and so \(\den{g}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\).
		\item \(g = \texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g'\))}\).
			\(|\sigma_1(\alpha)| \ge \#_\alpha g(\Delta) \; \forall \alpha \in \Delta\), so \(|\sigma_1(\alpha)| \ge \#_\alpha g'(\Delta,\tau) \; \forall \alpha \in \Delta\).
			By lemma \ref{lem:eqpat-extend}, for any \(\sigma_1(\Delta) \vdash v_1 : \sigma_1(\tau)\),
			there exists \(\sigma_2(\Delta) \vdash v_2 : \sigma_2(\tau)\) where \(\delta_1, x \mapsto v_1 \eqpat_{\Delta, \tau} \delta_2, x \mapsto v_2\).
			Similarly, \(|\sigma_2(\alpha)| \ge \#_\alpha g'(\Delta,\tau) \; \forall \alpha \in \Delta\), so for any \(\sigma_2(\Delta) \vdash v_2 : \sigma_2(\tau)\),
			there exists \(\sigma_1(\Delta) \vdash v_1 : \sigma_1(\tau)\) where \(\delta_1, x \mapsto v_1 \eqpat_{\Delta, \tau} \delta_2, x \mapsto v_2\).
			Thus \(\den{\texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g'\))}}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = w_1 + \dots + w_n\),
			and \(\den{\texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g'\))}}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) = w'_1 + \dots + w'_m\),
			where each weight \(w_1, \dots, w_n\) appears at least once in \(w'_1, \dots, w'_m\),
			and each weight \(w'_1, \dots, w'_m\) appears at least once in \(w_1, \dots, w_n\).
			Let \(r_1, \dots, r_k\) be the unique weights appearing in both sums.
			We require the semiring to be commutative, so we can group identical weights together.
			We therefore have: \(\den{\texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g'\))}}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = (r_1 + \dots) + \dots + (r_k + \dots)\),
			and \(\den{\texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g'\))}}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) = (r_1 + \dots\dots) + \dots + (r_k + \dots\dots)\).
			We require \(+\) to be idempotent.
			Thus: \(\den{\texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g'\))}}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = r_1 + \dots + r_k = \den{\texttt{(fresh ((\(x : \tau\))) \(g'\))}}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\)
		\item \(g = \texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}\).
			By lemma \ref{lem:eqpat-substitution}, we either have \(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) = \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\) and \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) = \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\),
			or \(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) \ne \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\) and \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) \ne \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\).
			In the first case, \(\den{\texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = 1\) and \(\den{\texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) = 1\).
			In the second case, \(\den{\texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = 0\) and \(\den{\texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) = 0\).
			In either case, \(\den{g}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\).
		\item \(g = \texttt{(=/= \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}\).
			By lemma \ref{lem:eqpat-substitution}, we either have \(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) = \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\) and \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) = \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\),
			or \(\den{v_1}(\delta_1) \ne \den{v_2}(\delta_1)\) and \(\den{v_1}(\delta_2) \ne \den{v_2}(\delta_2)\).
			In the first case, \(\den{\texttt{(=/= \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = 0\) and \(\den{\texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) = 0\).
			In the second case, \(\den{\texttt{(=/= \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = 1\) and \(\den{\texttt{(== \(v_1\) \(v_2\))}}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2) = 1\).
			In either case, \(\den{g}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = \den{g}(\gamma' \delta_2; \sigma_2)\).
		\item \(g = \texttt{(\(R\) \(v\) \(\dots\))}\).
			% TODO: clarify this case
			We assume relation calls work as expected.
		\item \(g = \texttt{(factor \(k\))}\).
			\(\den{\texttt{(factor \(k\))}}(\gamma; \delta_1; \sigma_1) = k = \den{\texttt{(factor \(k\))}}(\gamma; \delta_2; \sigma_2)\)
	\end{itemize}
\end{proof}
